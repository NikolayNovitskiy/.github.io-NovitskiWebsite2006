fMRI acoustic noise as a psychophysiological phenomenon

Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) proved to be indispensable in the sound processing investigation due to the powerful spatial resolution. However, loud acoustic noise produced by the magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scanner represents a problem in auditory fMRI research (Moelker and Pattynama, 2003). Besides the direct information on noise effects, fMRI noise research is useful for comparison of fMRI data with electroencephalographic and magnetoencephalographic data (EEG and MEG). Our previous studies (Novitski et al., 2001; Novitski et al., 2003) have shown that fMRI acoustic noise has a suppressing effect on electric event-related potentials. The early sensory components of ERP were reduced more than later cognitive ERPs in both active and passive auditory discrimination paradigms. In our recent study (Novitski et al., submitted) we investigated the interaction between the frequency range of stimulation and fMRI acoustic noise by recording magnetoencephalographic (MEG) responses to pitch change in silence and in noisy background. fMRI acoustic noise reduced amplitude of event-related magnetic fields. The effect of noise was more prominent for sinusoidal tones of high (>500 Hz) frequencies, which overlap with spectral maximum of the noise. Right supratemporal region was activated earlier than right frontal region in silent, but not in noise condition. We conclude that for a complete comparison of fMRI data with EEG or MEG data it might be advisable to choose stimuli which are separated spectrally from the fMRI scanner noise. The application of so called “sparse” imaging design (Hall et al., 1999) with temporal separation between noise and target acoustical stimulation is highly recommended in fMRI studies.
